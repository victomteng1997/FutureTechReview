## Overview

Bitcoin broker apps often compete on simplicity. That can be useful for first purchases, recurring buys, and mobile portfolio tracking. The tradeoff is that simple interfaces may hide custody terms, spreads, withdrawal limits, tax reporting gaps, and regional restrictions.

This checklist is for evaluating broker apps before using one as a primary on-ramp. It is informational review content, not financial, legal, tax, or investment advice.

## Custody and withdrawals

The first question is whether the app allows real Bitcoin withdrawals to a self-custody wallet. Some broker-style products offer price exposure without flexible withdrawal support. Others allow withdrawals, but only after identity checks, settlement delays, minimum amounts, or network fee limits.

If self-custody matters, test the full path with a small amount: deposit, buy, wait for settlement, withdraw, and confirm receipt. A product should make the custody model clear before a user commits meaningful funds.

## Fees and recurring buys

Broker apps may charge explicit fees, spreads, card fees, withdrawal fees, or conversion costs. Recurring buy users should review the total cost over many small purchases, not just the first transaction. A low-friction app with a high spread can become expensive over time.

Reporting also matters. Look for transaction history exports, cost basis data, statements, and clear timestamps. Even a simple Bitcoin-only strategy can create tax and accounting work if records are hard to download.

## Review checklist

- Confirm whether Bitcoin withdrawals are supported in your region.
- Compare explicit fees, spread, funding fees, and withdrawal costs.
- Test a small purchase and withdrawal before scaling up.
- Review recurring buy controls and cancellation flow.
- Export transaction history and verify the fields are useful.
- Read account closure, support, and custody terms.

## Final take

The best broker app is not necessarily the one with the fewest buttons. It is the one that lets a user understand custody, total cost, and exit options. Simplicity should reduce confusion without hiding the details that matter.
